Pandavleni Caves

Pandavleni Caves,Carved into the rugged slopes of Trirashmi Hill, about 8 km southwest of Nashik, the Pandavleni Caves are a collection of around 24 rock-cut sanctuaries that span from the 1st century BCE to the 3rd century CE, with further additions extending up to the 6th century CE. These caves primarily represent the Hinayana phase of Buddhism, focusing on simplicity, meditation spaces, and community living rather than elaborate iconography. The most prominent among them is Cave 3 (Gautamiputra Vihara), commissioned by Queen Gautami Balasiri, mother of the renowned Satavahana ruler Gautamiputra Satakarni around 150 CE. This grand vihara features spacious assembly halls, stone benches, intricately carved doorways, and detailed Brahmi inscriptions that shed light on the patronage of merchants, monks, and Satavahana rulers. These inscriptions not only mention significant political figures but also reveal the active participation of local trading guilds and spiritual communities in the site's development.
Cave 18 stands out as the sole chaitya-griha within the complex, housing a carefully crafted stupa and supported by elegantly carved pillars with chaitya-arch motifs, which showcase the devotional architectural style of early Buddhist worship. The caves’ advanced rock-cut cisterns and water-harvesting systems are a testament to the remarkable engineering skills of the period, ensuring a steady water supply even during dry seasons, which was essential for sustaining the monastic life at this remote hilltop retreat. Positioned high above the surrounding plains, the caves offer panoramic views of Nashik city and the Godavari river valley, creating an atmosphere of peace, isolation, and spiritual elevation.

Quick Facts

Location:
Nashik, Maharashtra.
History:
2nd Century BCE to 2nd Century CE, Buddhist site.
Main Attraction:
Viharas, Chaityas, and inscriptions of Satavahana kings.
Unique Design:
Stone-cut cells with finely carved doors and pillars.

Activities

  • Explore Cave 3 – a large vihara with intricately carved pillars and inscriptions
  • Visit the Buddhist prayer halls (Chaityas) and monastic cells (Viharas) carved into the basalt rock
  • Observe ancient Brahmi inscriptions and donor records from the 1st century BCE to 3rd century CE
  • Hike up the steps early morning for a peaceful sunrise and cool breeze
  • Capture stunning views of Nashik city and the Godavari River from the hilltop
