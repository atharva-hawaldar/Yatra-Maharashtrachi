BOR TIGER RESERVE

Bor Tiger Reserve, ocated near Hingani village in Wardha district, Maharashtra, is a lesser-known but ecologically rich wildlife sanctuary that offers a peaceful and uncrowded alternative to the more famous tiger reserves of central India. Spread across an area of approximately 138 square kilometers, Bor was officially declared a tiger reserve in 2014, becoming one of the newer additions to India’s Project Tiger network. The reserve is part of the Satpura-Maikal tiger landscape, serving as an important wildlife corridor that connects major tiger habitats like Pench, Melghat, and Tadoba.
The reserve’s core area, about 61 square kilometers, is dense and biologically significant, while the buffer zones provide critical support for both wildlife and nearby human settlements. Bor’s landscape features a harmonious mix of dry deciduous forests, grasslands, rocky outcrops, and the prominent Bor Reservoir, which serves as a vital water source for both animals and surrounding communities.
Although smaller than many other tiger reserves, Bor is known for its high tiger density relative to its size. Tigers frequently move between Bor and neighboring sanctuaries, contributing to genetic diversity and population stability in the region. In addition to tigers, Bor Tiger Reserve is home to leopards, sloth bears, wild boars, Indian bison (gaur), nilgai, chital, sambar, barking deer, and langurs. Bor is also a delight for birdwatchers and nature enthusiasts, with more than 160 recorded species of birds, including the Indian roller, crested serpent eagle, kingfishers, bee-eaters, and various species of storks and herons that thrive around the reservoir and forest clearings. The calm waters of the Bor Dam attract many migratory birds in the winter, making it a seasonal hotspot for bird photography.

Quick Facts

Location:
Wardha District, near Hingani village, Maharashtra.
Founded:
Declared as a tiger reserve in 2014.
Best Time to Visit:
October to June; peak sightings February to May.
Entry Fees:
Entry and safari fees apply at Bor gate.

Activities

  • Go on a *Jeep Safari* from the Bordharan or Adegaon gates in early morning or late afternoon to spot Bengal tigers, leopards, sloth bears, deer, wild dogs, and abundant birdlife
  • Walk or cycle through buffer‑zone trails alongside the Bor Reservoir—ideal for encountering sambars, peafowl, langurs, and migratory waterbirds
  • Visit the *eco‑tourism complex* near Bor Dam, and stay in MTDC cottages or forest lodges offering nature dining and scenic views
  • Explore nearby *heritage temples* such as Khori‑Khapa Shiva Temple, Bruhaspati Temple at Chauki, Hanuman Temple at Khadki, and Kelzar Ganesh Temple
  • Participate in *birdwatching and wildlife photography*, with over 160 bird species (including endangered vultures) and 26 reptile species plus diverse mammals
