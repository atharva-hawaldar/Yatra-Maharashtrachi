PENCH NATIONAL PARK

Pench National Park, straddling the border of Maharashtra and Madhya Pradesh, is one of India’s most celebrated tiger reserves, known for its rich biodiversity and its inspiration for Rudyard Kipling’s classic, The Jungle Book. Officially established as a wildlife sanctuary in 1965 and declared a national park in 1975, Pench gained further recognition when it was designated a tiger reserve in 1992 under India’s Project Tiger. The park covers a total area of approximately 758 square kilometers, with about 257 square kilometers forming the core zone and the rest as buffer areas spread across both states.
The park’s landscape is a picturesque blend of undulating hills, dense teak forests, open grasslands, and meandering streams, with the Pench River, from which the park gets its name, flowing right through its heart. The river and its seasonal tributaries are lifelines that sustain the diverse flora and fauna throughout the year. The ecosystem here supports a rich variety of wildlife, making it one of the most ecologically balanced parks in central India.
Pench National Park is best known for its thriving tiger population. It is home to an estimated 50-60 tigers, along with a healthy population of other predators such as leopards, wild dogs (dholes), jungle cats, and hyenas. The park is equally renowned for its herbivore population, including large herds of chital (spotted deer), sambar, nilgai, and gaur (Indian bison), all of which support the predator-prey dynamic of the forest. The presence of the majestic Indian leopard adds an extra thrill to wildlife safaris.The park is also a birdwatcher’s paradise, boasting over 250 species of resident and migratory birds, including peafowls, hornbills, crested serpent eagles, and various species of kingfishers, storks, and waterfowl that thrive around the riverbanks and forest clearings. Reptiles, insects, and smaller mammals like civets and palm squirrels contribute to the park’s complex food web.

Quick Facts

Location:
Border of Maharashtra and Madhya Pradesh.
Founded:
National park since 1975, tiger reserve since 1992.
Best Time to Visit:
October to June; best sightings from February to April.
Entry Fees:
Entry fees and safari vehicle charges apply per gate.

Activities

  • Go on a Jeep Safari in early morning or late afternoon shifts to spot Bengal tigers, leopards, deer, sloth bears, wild boar, and a variety of birds
  • Join a Night (Wolf) Safari in the buffer zone to spot nocturnal creatures like wolves, jackals, sambar, nilgai, and enjoy stargazing in a serene forest setting
  • Walk the Runi Jhuni nature trail through the Karmajhiri zone for a close-up look at wildlife, tracks, birds, and local flora
  • Cycle through the Rukhad buffer zone (up to ~22 km), ending with optional camping in forest terrain and wildlife spotting en route
  • Visit the nearby Pachdhar “Potter Village” to learn traditional pottery craft, interact with artisans, and shop for handmade earthen items
